Round duct vs rectangular duct

Choose the shape from the complete route, available space, area, fittings, construction, and pressure budget—not from a single efficiency claim.

Created 2026-07-16 · Updated 2026-07-16

Key differences

FactorRound ductRectangular duct
Route geometryNeeds circular clearance.Can fit shallow or wide chases.
Area checkArea follows diameter directly.Area follows width × height; aspect ratio also matters.
Friction comparisonCan be checked from diameter and flow.Use an equivalent-duct or documented design method.
FittingsStill needs elbow, takeoff, and transition checks.Still needs elbow, takeoff, transition, and aspect-ratio checks.
Decision basisRequired airflow, actual velocity, pressure budget, route, construction, leakage control, and installation access.

Example: compare actual area, not only width

A rectangular duct that fits a shallow ceiling may be the practical route even when a round duct has a simpler diameter check. Calculate the actual free area and velocity for the selected geometry, then evaluate equivalent diameter or friction behavior as appropriate. A shape that fits physically can still create a pressure or sound problem if the entire path is not checked.

When each option is often practical

  • Round can be convenient where depth and access are available and a simple diameter-based layout fits.
  • Rectangular can be practical in constrained ceiling cavities, shafts, or wide shallow routes.
  • Either option needs the same discipline: assigned airflow, actual area, velocity, fittings, friction, fan performance, and balancing.

FAQ

Which duct shape is always more efficient?

Neither shape is universally better. Route, size, fittings, aspect ratio, construction, leakage control, and fan pressure determine system performance.

Can two ducts with the same face area be treated as identical?

No. Geometry and fittings can change pressure loss and installation behavior. Use an appropriate equivalent-diameter or detailed duct-design method when comparing them.
