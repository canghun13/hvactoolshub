Air changes per hour explained

ACH expresses an identified airflow as a multiple of an enclosed room’s volume each hour. It is useful for a room-volume comparison, but it does not identify the air source or prove performance.

Created 2026-07-16 · Updated 2026-07-20

What ACH means

ACH is hourly volume flow divided by an enclosed room volume. It offers a compact way to compare nominal air movement between rooms of different sizes. Label the flow before calculating: supply, return, outdoor air, exhaust, and recirculated air have different roles.

Formula

ACH = (CFM × 60) ÷ room volume (ft³)

For metric inputs, m³/h divided by room volume in m³ gives ACH directly. Use one consistent unit system rather than mixing CFM with cubic metres.

Worked example

A 2,400 ft³ room supplied with 240 CFM has 240 × 60 ÷ 2,400 = 6 ACH. With the same 240 CFM in a 4,800 ft³ room, ACH is 3. The fan flow did not change; only the reference volume did.

Use ACH in the right workflow

  1. Identify the room boundary and calculate or measure its volume.
  2. Identify the air stream and why it is being evaluated.
  3. Use the formula to convert a known CFM to ACH, or a stated ACH basis to CFM.
  4. For ventilation requirements, use the applicable standard, code, and project design basis.
  5. For heating and cooling, verify the load, supply-air conditions, distribution, ducts, and fan capability separately.

FAQ

Is one ACH a complete air replacement?

No. ACH is a nominal flow-to-volume relationship. Mixing, short-circuiting, distribution, leakage, and outdoor-air fraction can be different.

Can ACH be used for duct sizing?

It can yield a preliminary CFM after a room volume and explicit ACH basis are known. Duct design still requires actual area, velocity, fittings, pressure loss, and fan checks.

Can ACH size HVAC equipment?

No. Capacity should follow a documented heating or cooling load and design conditions. ACH does not capture those inputs.
